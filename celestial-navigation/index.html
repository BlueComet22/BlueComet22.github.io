Human travel and the stars.

Humans never really stopped travelling during the tens of thousands of years that we’ve been around, whether it be to explore an unfamiliar place or to return to a place they know. In those thousands of years, the way that we’ve navigated our travels has changed greatly; however—

—we often look up to the sky to create them.

Before GPSs, sea travelers depended on the stars to know where they were and what direction that they were going in. Those methods are what we call celestial navigation. It is perhaps not as dependable as GPS, but with a lot of effort, it was accurate enough, and it definitely helped when there weren’t that many options to begin with.

These seven stars in the sky create the little Dipper, also commonly known as Ursa Minor, stars in the shape of a small bear. This is one of the most popular constellations for many reasons, including in celestial navigation. This bright star on its tail is called Polaris, also known as the ‘north star’ or the ‘pole star’; it’s the brightest star in Ursa Minor and visible to the naked eye at night. It’s also a star that’s so close to the north pole that no matter what day or time of year it is in the northern hemisphere it doesn’t fall below the horizon. In fact—

It doesn’t really move all that much in the sky either, especially compared to the other stars; with a constant like this, it’s a great method of finding where you are regarding your latitude – that is, the angle you are at from the equator.

Celestial Navigation

Draco

Polaris wasn’t always the North Star, however; 1600 years ago was a diffeent story.

It used to be Thuban, this star in the Draco constellation. But the North Star changed, like all things eventually do. Either way, that doesn't change that the North Star makes it nice and easy to find your latitude, relatively speaking. But. Wait. What about the longitude?

You can’t find a location without longitude. That’s like being given a street without the avenue. Now, how do we calculate that? Unfortunately—we can’t use Polaris to find the longitude. But there are many other stars in these constellations that can be used in its stead.

Using these, you can find the lunar distance, which is the angle between the moon and one of these stars on the ecliptic; that of which is the plane about here, where the orbits of the planets go straight across and have no angle. With that and a fair bit of math using a special table, you can find the longitude. The good news, though, is that in celestial navigation in more recent time, we can use a marine chronometer, or a special clock to use on boats and find the difference between the time zone you’re in and Coordinated Universal Time.

At the end of the day, though, not many of us get to use something like this, especially not in our daily lives. What we do oft use, however, are these.

These are GPS satellites, something that you may very often use on your cellphone or your computer when you need directions from a place or are very, very lost. There number and their movements allow for one to locate themselves on the entire globe with just a GPS receiver. To get a GPS to work, you need only four visible satellites at your location, and many locations average at 7 to 10 at any given time, though with some hiccups if the signal ends up being blocked wherever you may be, since the signals are weak. While it’s certainly a lot easier to understand the way that the information is handed to you on your device. That doesn’t mean that celestial navigation is no longer done.

Seeing this progression of our methods, of how our methods of navigation has changed, are simply a testemant to our technological advancements.
